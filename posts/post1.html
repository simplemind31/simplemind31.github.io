I started doing math olympiads in 2022. Back then, everything felt simple in a na√Øve way: a problem, a pencil, a blank sheet of paper, and time. I didn't think about teams, international contests, or medals. I just liked the feeling of thinking hard about something and, after struggling for a while, finally understanding it.

Little by little, I started getting closer to bigger things. By 2024, I was already part of Bolivian teams for some international math olympiads and competitions. I thought math was ‚Äúit‚Äù for me, until 2023, when I discovered Olympiad in Informatics.

Discovering Informatics

OI felt different from the very first problems I saw. It still had the depth and rigor of math, but now ideas had to survive contact with reality. You could have a beautiful idea and still fail because of an implementation detail. You had to think, but you also had to build. For me, that combination, problem solving plus code, was fascinating. It felt more alive.

When the selection process for IOI 2024 started in late 2023, I decided to focus more seriously on Informatics. That year, our selection was based on accumulated scores across many contests. Sometimes I felt annoying. Sometimes I felt slow. But looking back, that persistence, and Diego's patience, taught me an incredible amount. My background in math helped me understand ideas faster, but Informatics forced me to be precise in a way I had never been before.

Eventually, I earned a spot for IOI 2024. I remember feeling proud, relieved, and excited, all at once. And then reality hit. IOI 2024 was held in Egypt, and the cost was too high. My dad didn't want me to go. Bolivia ended up sending only three people that year.

OII and Pressure

I won't lie: it hurt. It hurt to earn something and then have to give it up. I still feel a bit sorry for Mati, because technically I took a spot I couldn't use. At least I was able to participate in OII, the Iberoamerican Olympiad in Informatics. That was my first international OI experience, and honestly, I didn't do well. I only got an honorable mention.

I think pressure beat me there. Some contestants who ranked lower than me in the selection performed better at OII. That taught me something uncomfortable: being ‚Äúbetter on paper‚Äù doesn't mean much when nerves take control.

OBI and the Long Road to IOI 2025

When the others went to Egypt, I stayed behind. I was sad, but I didn't stop. In late 2024, OBI, our national Olympiad, was approaching, and this time I wasn't competing with beginners anymore. I trained harder than ever. OBI that year was extremely tight: I finished second, just one minute behind first place. Third place was only a few minutes behind me. For the first time, I felt how cruel and precise time could be.

OBI 2024

OBI 2024

The real beginning of IOI 2025 started in October 2024, when school ended and vacation began. IOI was going to be held in Bolivia, and that changed everything. As hosts, we felt an unspoken responsibility: we had to do our best.

Our coach selected the four strongest past contestants to focus on. This didn't mean we were already in the team, it meant pressure. Contest after contest, problem after problem. Our vacation slowly disappeared into statements and wrong submissions. It was frustrating. I think we all suffered. That year, I almost completely stopped focusing on math olympiads and put all my energy into Informatics.

The Training Camp

In March, something important happened: after years without one (probably because of COVID), we finally had a training camp. Five contests in one week. Classes. Living together. Eating together. Complaining together. That camp decided the ten Bolivian participants for OII, but more importantly, it made us a team. That one week was exhausting, and unforgettable.

Final Selection and Preparation

After the camp came the final contest in April. Its results, combined with the camp, determined who would go to IOI. As hosts, Bolivia had eight spots: four official and four unofficial. We all pushed ourselves as hard as we could. When the results came out, I earned a place.

Training didn't stop. In fact, it got heavier. Our coach started simulating real IOIs with past problems. Then, something unexpected happened: about a week before IOI, he stopped giving us problems entirely. He even prohibited us from solving anything for the last two weeks. He told us to rest.

Strangely, I felt confident. Not because I thought I was amazing, but because we had already gone through the worst.

IOI Week

As IOI week approached, Sucre began to feel different. I would see groups of foreigners walking down the street, and I could almost immediately tell they were IOI contestants. They looked scary, not in a bad way, just‚Ä¶ smart. My nerves went up every time.

The day finally arrived. Since IOI was in Sucre, I just took a taxi to the venue. Suddenly, I was surrounded by people from all over the world. I learned card games, laughed a lot, and slowly forgot about the competition itself. Team 1 shared rooms with Belgium, team 2 with the USA, eight people per room, two countries mixed together. At some point, it felt less like a contest and more like a strange, beautiful gathering of people who loved the same thing.

Keyboards exploded. Pages flipped. Pens moved. I made a mistake right away: I started with problem 1. I spent two or three hours stuck, unable to find a clean approach. Eventually, I moved to problems 2 and 3, tried to squeeze partial points, and returned to problem 1 near the end.

But the rest of the day was different. We played games, solved puzzles from sponsors: Huawei, OpenAI, CodeMao, Tech Arena, Tencent. We collected plushies, pens, shirts, bags‚Ä¶ and somehow, happiness. For a moment, I forgot about my score.

Between the Days

The next day was a tour. In the afternoon, we held the OII award ceremony. Even though we already knew the results, it was special to finally receive the medals. I got silver, so did Gabriel. Mati, Agus and Nestor got bronze.

OII Award Ceremony ‚Äî silver, bronze, and shared pride.

That afternoon, our coach gathered us and said some motivational words for day 2. My teammates looked confident. I didn't. I cried, I felt useless, I complained about what I could have done on day 1. My best friend (Nico) tried to calm me down, but even on the bus back to the hotel, I was still angry at myself.

Nico trying to motivate me.

Contest Day 2

Day 2 came. Same breakfast. Same hall. Same countdown. I won't describe the contest again, the feeling was identical.

When it ended, my total score was 207.29 out of 600. I hoped that it would be enough for bronze. Then our leader told me: ‚ÄúYour ranking is the same as your score.‚Äù

I knew I had failed.

Some people congratulated me for breaking Bolivia's best historical ranking and score. I couldn't feel happy. My goal was a medal. I was 45 points away. In a perfect world, a world where I fixed that problem 3, it was possible.

I think I saw disappointment in my coach's eyes. That hurt more than my score. I felt like we had failed him.

Endings

After that, time moved strangely. We played, talked, laughed. The event slowly came to an end. I made friends, real ones, from Belgium, Macau, the USA, and of course, my own team. We loved the same thing. That was enough.

That last night, Agust√≠n left quietly with his father. He packed his things without saying much. I don't think he wanted us to notice. When the door closed, the room felt suddenly smaller, as if something invisible had been taken away. I lay there staring at the ceiling, thinking about how strange it is that you don't realize something is ending until it already has.

I didn't want to go back home. Not to school, not to homework, not to a routine where no one understands why a wrong idea can ruin your whole day or why a partial score can haunt you for weeks. For the first time, it crossed my mind that this might be my last time living this life, the life of contests, camps, scoreboards, and shared exhaustion.

I know my dad. Once I start college, he won't let me keep traveling for olympiads. He sees it as a chapter that should end. And maybe he's right. But that night, lying in that room, it felt heavy to accept that IOI 2025 might be my last dance in Olympiad Informatics.

The next morning arrived too fast. The room felt emptier than it had a week before. Everyone was packing, folding clothes, checking passports, saying quick goodbyes that felt too small for what we had shared. I wasn't hungry. I didn't know what to say. Soon the bus to the airport arrived, and one by one, everyone disappeared. I took a taxi back home, alone, carrying more than just a suitcase.

I came back without a medal. But what hurt wasn't the empty hands, it was leaving a place where I felt understood. A place where people spoke the same language. A place where I belonged without having to explain myself.

If this really was my last IOI, then I'm grateful it ended like this. With friends from all over the world. With memories that still hurt when I think about them. With the certainty that, for a moment in my life, I gave everything I had to something I loved.

Maybe I didn't get the medal I wanted.

But I got a goodbye worth remembering.

Hope

At the beginning of January, more precisely, on January 4th, 2026, my phone vibrated. It was a normal moment, nothing special. I unlocked the screen and read the message.

Diego:
‚ÄúOne more IOI? I'll look for ways to pay your trip to Uzbekistan.‚Äù

I stayed still for a few seconds. It felt unreal. After everything, after saying goodbye in my head, after convincing myself that IOI 2025 was the end, suddenly there was a door half-open again.

I really wanted to say yes. Not carefully, not politely. Just yes. One more chance. One last shot. But fear came faster than excitement. I know my parents. I know how these conversations usually end.

So I replied:

‚ÄúSounds great, but I don't know if my parents will let me go :(‚Äù

That message carried more than it showed.

I'm afraid because, somehow, my achievements never feel enough at home. When I get a bronze medal, the first question is who got gold and how far I was from them. When I ace an exam, they say scores don't matter. But when I do poorly, scores suddenly matter a lot. Coming back from IOI 2025, they didn't see the best rank and score Bolivia had ever achieved. They only saw that first place was close to 600 and I was far from that.

This year I'm supposed to start college in the US, if I get my visa. IOI 2026 would likely collide with my first days of class. In my head, the answer was already no. I assumed it. I prepared myself for it.

And that's what hurt the most.

I spent almost all of 2025 chasing a medal I didn't get. And now that someone was offering me one more chance, not guaranteed, not easy, but real, I couldn't take it unless my parents allowed me to. It felt unfair in a quiet, exhausting way.

That afternoon I didn't know what to do. I went to my room, closed the door, and cried. I called Nico and complained about everything, the timing, the pressure, the feeling of being close but never enough. I kept thinking that for any competitive programmer, being invited to one more IOI is a dream. And there I was, hesitating.

Eventually, I calmed down and asked Diego if he could talk to my parents. He said yes, but not that day. So I waited. I hoped.

When that day finally came, something unexpected happened.

My dad, the one who almost always says no, said yes this time (later he told me that he thought IOI was going to be held in US, so lol).

I didn't know how to react. It felt fragile, like something that could disappear if I celebrated too early. My mom, however, was still unsure. She worries about college, about immigration rules, about whether I could leave the US and re-enter with a student visa. I understand her fears. I really do.

So now I'm here, in the selection process again. Not celebrating, not dreaming too loudly. Just hoping. Hoping that things align. Hoping that I get the chance. Hoping that this door, which opened so quietly, doesn't close before I can step through it.

If IOI 2025 was my goodbye, then IOI 2026 feels like a question mark.

And for now, that question mark is enough to keep me moving.
